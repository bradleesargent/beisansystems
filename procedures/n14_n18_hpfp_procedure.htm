Introduction

An automotive high pressure fuel pump is a device that generates very high fuel pressure for the engine. The fuel tank electric fuel pump pressurizes fuel to ~4 bar (~60 psi). A high pressure fuel pump further pressurizes this fuel to ~200 bar (~3k psi). This high pressure fuel is used for engine direct fuel injection (inside cylinder fuel injection), and facilitates high precision fuel control for optimal engine function and efficient fuel consumption. A high pressure fuel pump is a mechanical device, as high mechanical forces are needed to produce such high fuel pressures. It is normally connected to and driven by the engine.

The N14/N18 high pressure fuel pump is a rotary type pump that utilizes hydraulic fluid (oil) for the pumping action. The rotor on the pump, which is driven by the engine, is connected to and turns a swash plate (slanted disk). As the swash plate rotates it causes the reciprocation of hollow spring loaded pistons in/out of cylinders. The hydraulic fluid filled pistons expand and deflate bellows (balloons) as they pump and then release hydraulic fluid in/out of the bellows. The bellows expansion presses on fuel, in a fuel chamber, causing a check valve to open and accept the pressurized fuel into the high pressure fuel rail. The bellows deflation draws fuel, in the same fuel chamber, causing a second check valve to open and allow in new unpressurized fuel from the low pressure fuel supply. There are 3 sets of pumping piston, spring, cylinder, bellow, fuel chamber, in/out check valves. An electronic solenoid, driven by a PWM (pulse width modulation) signal, controls the fuel flow into the pump and thus in turn the high pressure fuel output of the pump into the high pressure fuel rail.

The N14/N18 HPFP (high pressure fuel pump) experiences one primary failure. The bellows’ upper collar incorporates a small O-ring which separates the fuel chamber from the pump hydraulic fluid chamber. This O-ring fails and allows fuel to seep into the hydraulic fluid chamber. The hydraulic fluid then becomes contaminated and this changes its functional characteristics which causes the HPFP to malfunction. The contaminated hydraulic fluid becomes excessively thin and this allows it to leak past the pistons when the pistons are pumped to fill and expand the bellows to pressurize the fuel. As the hydraulic fluid becomes more and more degraded, the pump fuel pressure further and further reduces, eventually causing notable engine performance symptoms.

The bellow O-ring failure is due to degradation from high pressure fuel. Although the HPFP housing is designed to expose the bellow O-ring to minimal fuel pressure, in application the O-ring is still exposed to levels of fuel pressure it cannot endure. The O-ring experiences two degradations. Primarily, it experiences significant compression set (permanent flattening). This reduces the O-ring sealing force and allows leakage. Secondarily, the O-ring loses material, either from shredding or from abrasion, depending on the O-ring material, and this further allows leakage. It is also likely the O-ring low temperature operation limit is also a failure point. At temperatures below operating temperature limit an O-ring rubber will not seal well and will allow leakage.

The bellow O-ring has been redesigned multiple times by BMW (ContiTech). Beisan has found 4 O-ring designs from inspecting used failed HPFPs.
The following is a list of the O-rings, 1-4, oldest to newest design, with a description of the O-ring and its seeming failure mode.

O-ring 1 is a standard O-ring design and is likely made from Viton 75 durometer (hardness). It is relatively thin and thus will easily leak from moderate compression set created from the fuel high pressure.
O-ring 2 is a custom design O-ring and is likely made from Viton 70 durometer. Although the design is seemingly quite good, the low durometer makes is susceptible to compression set and shredding form the fuel high pressure.
O-ring 3 is a standard design O-ring and is likely made from Viton 75 durometer. It is relatively thick and is cut when installed. It has the benefit of better filling the bellow O-ring cavity. But it also experiences compression set and shredding.
O-ring 4 is a standard design O-ring and is likely made from Fluorosilicone 75 durometer. It is relatively thin and experiences significant compression set and material loss from abrasion. The change in material from Viton to Fluorosilicone, which is a combination of Viton and Silicon, is an indication the design change was made to address low temperature operation, as Viton operates down to -15F (-26C) and Fluorosilicone operates down to -70F (-56C).

The following is a discussion of the bellow O-ring needed features and the BMW O-rings design failures.

Viton (fluorocarbon, FKM) is an almost optimal O-ring material. It is resistant to most chemicals and has an upper operating temperature of 400F (200C). It has the best compression set resistance of all the O-ring rubber materials. It is not susceptible to tearing or abrasion. Its only weak point is a lower operating temperature of -15F (-26C). But of recent times, rubber manufacturers are offering Viton versions that operate down to -24F (-31C) and -40F (-40C), reducing the fluorine content from 69% to 67%. BMW used Fluorosilicone rubber instead of low temperature Viton to address cold region requirements. But Fluorosilicone is significantly more susceptible to compression set (flattening) and abrasion than Viton. A better choice of material would have been low temperature Viton.

Standard rubber durometer (hardness) is 75 Shore A scale. High pressure applications are known to cause O-ring leakage, compression set, and rubber shredding/abrasion. The solution is the use of high durometer rubber. A high durometer rubber provides higher compression forces to prevent leakage, significantly lower compression set (flattening), and higher resistance to shredding/abrasion. Viton is available in a high hardness 90 durometer. BMW did not use 90 durometer rubber with any of the bellow O-ring versions.

Pressure is measured in force per square area. Thus minimizing the O-ring area exposed to the high fuel pressure will minimize the forces on the O-ring and in turn degradation. The BMW bellow O-rings are not only experiencing ID (inner diameter) radial compression set (flattening), they are also experiencing one sided axial compression set. This side compression set is an indication of fuel entering the bellow O-ring cavity and pressing against the side of the O-ring. A normal design round O-ring allows for this, but a custom design O-ring, like the BMW bellow O-ring 2 with flat sides, can fill the bellow O-ring cavity space and prevent the fuel filling and pressing. Unfortunately BMW used a low durometer rubber on the O-ring 2 design and this caused its failure.

Contemporary fuels are blends with ethanol/methanol and this can be corrosive to O-ring rubber. Viton is now available in ethanol/methanol resistant versions.

An N14/N18 HPFP seals repair kit and associate HPFP tools can be acquired from Beisan Systems, www.beisansystems.com. The bellow O-ring is made from Viton, 90 durometer, -40F (-40C) operation, ethanol/methanol resistant. The physical design has flat sides with width .02mm smaller than the bellow O-ring cavity, and arced ID & OD for compression force. The O-ring size is the maximum material fit. The design achieves full O-ring cavity fill with maximum rubber compression force. The solenoid O-rings are Viton, 80 durometer, -40F (-40C) operation, ethanol/methanol resistant. The dome and body O-rings are Viton 80 durometer. The rotary seal is Viton durometer 75 and is the same as the original part.

Repair Procedure

The following is a Mini Cooper S HPFP (high pressure fuel pump) rebuild R&R (remove and replace) procedure.

Repair time: 4 hours mechanic, 5+ hours DIY.

Repair

Car engine must be cold to perform repair procedure.

Right and Left denotations are from car front at hood orientation.

Rebuilding of HPFP

Perform following work on table to prevent parts from falling and being damaged.


Spray clean HPFP external body (brake cleaner / towels).

Removal of solenoid


Place HPFP on vise anvil with solenoid at top and rear (picture).
Rotate rotor to contact vise for better stability (picture).


Remove solenoid mounting bolts.
Remove 2 bolts (T25 torx bit socket 1/4" w/ 3/8” to 1/4” socket adapter / 3/8” ratchet).


Pry up and remove solenoid bracket.


Remove solenoid.
Wiggle, pry up, and remove solenoid (pick tool).


Remove fuel from bottom of solenoid cylinder.
Fold and roll towel and insert into solenoid cylinder to soak up fuel at bottom of cylinder (towels).

Removal of dome rotor


Open vise jaws to the limit.
Install soft vise jaw liners with horizontal notch liner on right and multiple notches liner on left (picture).


Mount HPFP in vise.
Orient HPFP with rotor at top and oil overflow pod at front left (picture).
Slide back left vise liner to be just behind left bolt mount protrusion.
Position HPFP to have little space between vise and oil overflow pod (picture).
Note: Space is needed to protect oil overflow pod.
Lay HPFP flat on vise base and verify it’s not tilted.
Strongly tighten vise jaws onto HPFP.


Mount bearing puller small base on rotor.
Center puller base with rotor sides and tighten base onto rotor bottom (picture).
Note: Base hand tightening is sufficient.


Mount bearing puller poles on bearing base (picture).
Note: Poles hand tightening is sufficient.


Mount bearing puller cross member with spindle on bearing puller poles (picture).
Place washers on poles at top and bottom of cross member (picture).
Mount nuts on poles to secure cross member (picture).
Note: Keep nuts slightly loose to allow spindle alignment in next step.


Thread bearing puller spindle down into rotor shaft (picture).
Shift bearing puller cross member as needed to align spindle with rotor center shaft.
Note: Verify spindle end is inside rotor center diameter and is not overlapping rotor.


Tighten bearing puller spindle to pull up on rotor (18mm socket 1/2" / 1/2" ratchet).
Counter hold puller cross member to keep puller from turning (hand).
Spindle will press into rotor center shaft and puller base will pull up and dislodge rotor from shaft.

Removal of dome brackets


Remove dome O-ring.
Press on O-ring from opposite sides to push O-ring out of cavity, then pull O-ring off dome (fingers).


Remove dome bracket mounting bolts.
Remove 3 dome bracket mounting bolts (T20 torx bit socket 1/4" w/ 3/8” to 1/4” socket adapter / 3/8” ratchet).


Pull up on and remove dome mounting bracket(s) (hands).

Removal of dome


Remount HPFP in vise.
Open vise jaws and remove HPFP.
Position vise liners center on vise jaws.
Insert HPFP in vise with dome down.
Position dome centered at left vise liner center notch (feel).
Position vise jaws on dome end flat section (picture).
Strongly tighten vise jaws onto HPFP dome.
Place folded towels in vise under dome (towels).


Remove HPFP body from HPFP dome.
Tilt side to side and pull HPFP body until it dislodges from dome.
Note: Dome is mounted to HPFP body by an O-ring, and this can be difficult to dislodge.
When dome is removed, piston parts will likely stick to done swash plate due to magnetized parts (picture).


Place piston parts in container.
Cover dome with towels from vise, and remove from vise and turn right side up to drain oil into towels.
Place dome with towels in container.


Remove HPFP body sealing O-ring from dome.
Press on O-ring from opposite sides to push O-ring out of cavity, then pull O-ring off dome (fingers).

Parts cleaning and removal of rotary seal


Disassemble and clean dome and piston parts (brake cleaner / towels).
Component inside dome is swashplate with shaft.
Piston components are piston, spring, and cap.

Spray clean HPFP cylinders and body (brake cleaner / towels).


Mount HPFP in vise.
Orient HPFP with cylinders at top and oil overflow pod at front left (picture).
Slide back left vise liner to be just behind left bolt mount protrusion.
Position HPFP to position overflow pod a small distance from vise (picture).
Note: Space is needed to protect oil overflow pod.
Lay HPFP flat on vise base and verify it’s not tilted.
Strongly tighten vise jaws onto HPFP.


Mount dome on HPFP body (picture).
Note: Dome mounting is temporary and is needed to hold dome for rotary seal removal in following step.


Remove rotary seal from dome.
Insert pry tool into rotary seal just under rotary seal top (3/8” chisel).
Hit down on end of pry tool to leverage against dome and pry out rotary seal from dome (2lb sledgehammer).

Remove dome from HPFP body.
Note: If dome if stuck on HPFP body, tilt from several direction to release.

Clean dome rotary seal cavity (brake cleaner / towels).

Removal of piston cylinders


Mount 4 pin socket onto a cylinder.
As needed, tilt socket from side to side to facilitate pins insertion.
Verify socket mates fully to cylinder face.
If socket not fully matted with cylinder, hit socket to further insert pins (2 lb sledgehammer).

Note: Socket must be fully seated onto cylinder face.


Remove HPFP cylinder.
Mount impact driver on 4 pin socket (impact driver / 1/2" socket adapter).
Set impact driver to reverse rotation.
Assure 4 pin socket is fully matted to cylinder face.
Engage impact drive to unscrew cylinder from HPFP.
Note: Engage impact driver for 3 seconds at a time until cylinder thread seize breaks.


Repeat above two steps to remove second cylinder.

Removal of bellows


Insert bellow puller (bolt/nut) into a bellow.
Thread puller nut onto puller bolt with nut hex oriented to bolt head (picture).
Thread nut most of the way onto bolt (picture).
Insert bolt straight into a bellow and turn slightly to initially engage bellow (picture).

Note: Puller will be threaded into bellow in next step.


Thread puller bolt into bellow.
Note: Bolt is tapered at end which allows threading into bellow head inner diameter.
Note: Bolt threading is very slight and does not affect bellow function.
Significantly press down on puller bolt head (hand palm) while rotating bolt head (adjustable wrench).
Perform bolt press and rotation 3 times to achieve an approximate full bolt rotation.
Note: Maintain bolt straightness and do not allow to tilt.


Remove bellow from HPFP body.
Thread puller nut down onto HPFP body (fingers).
Tighten bellow puller nut onto HPFP body until bellow pulls (pops) out of body (adjustable wrench).

Note: If puller bolt disconnects from bellow and does not remove bellow, repeat above step for remounting puller bolt into bellow and current step to pull bellow.


Remove puller bolt from bellow.
Thread puller nut down onto bellow (fingers).
Counter hold puller nut and unscrew puller bolt (adjustable wrench, 22mm 1/2" socket / 1/2" ratchet).


Repeat above 4 steps to remove second bellow.


Remove spring at bottom of each body cylinder (small medium-nose pliers).


Remove fuel from bottom of body cylinders.
Fold and roll towel and insert into each body cylinder to soak up fuel at bottom of cylinder (towels).


Remove O-ring from bellow.
Press in and push out on O-ring from opposite sides, and pull O-ring off bellow (picture).

Note: Do not use tools to remove bellow O-ring as this can scratch and damage bellow.


Repeat above O-ring removal step for second bellow.


Clean bellows O-ring cavity (brake cleaner / towels).

Note: If O-ring is yellow (Fluorosilicone), significant cleaning is needed to fully remove material.
Note: It is important to fully clean bellows’ O-ring cavity. Verify last cleaning has no material removed.


Clean body cylinders threads and O-ring matting surface (brake cleaner / towels).
Body cylinders O-ring matting surface is below threads and above ledge.

Note: If O-ring is yellow (Fluorosilicone), significant cleaning is needed to fully remove material.
Note: It is important to fully clean cylinders’ O-ring matting surface. Verify last cleaning has no material removed.

Installation of bellows


Install bellow springs in body cylinders (small medium-nose pliers).
Note: Bellow spring has identical ends and can be installed on either end.


Install new custom O-ring on bellow.
Insert O-ring (inner side) in bellow cavity on one side, then stretch O-ring and drop into cavity on opposite side (fingers).
Note: O-ring will need significant stretching to install.

If an O-ring section is twisted and protruding from cavity, press in and push out on O-ring on each side of problem section to lift out section and then drop back down straight into cavity.
Verify O-ring is fully seated in bellow cavity by holding bellow at O-ring and rotating bellow to feel any improper O-ring seating (fingers).
Note: It is critical that O-ring be installed fully straight and not twisted.


Repeat above O-ring installation step for second bellow.


Apply light lubricant to body cylinder bellow O-ring matting surface (oil / finger).
Body cylinder O-ring matting surface is below threads and above ledge.
Note: Be sure to lubricant slope just below threads.


Apply light lubricant to bellow O-ring (oil / fingers).


Insert lubricated bellow in lubricated body cylinder.
Note: Verify spring is mounted correctly in cylinder before inserting bellow.


Repeat above cylinder and bellow O-ring lubrication and insertion steps for second body cylinder and bellow.

Installation of cylinders and pistons

Spray clean cylinders (brake cleaner / towels).


Insert 2 cylinders inside 2 bellows.


Thread cylinders into HPFP body.
Tighten 2 cylinders (4 pin socket / 1/2” ratchet).
Note: Down pressure on cylinder is needed to engage first thread.
Note: Tightening of cylinders presses down and mounts bellows into body cylinders.
Half way into cylinder threading, notable resistance will be encountered. Resistance is due to compression of bellow O-ring as it is pressed into its tighter mount location. Higher force is needed to complete tightening.


Fully tighten 2 piston cylinders, 55 Nm (40.5 ft-lb) (4 pin socket / 3/8” torque wrench & 3/8” to 1/2" socket adapter).

Clean HPFP cylinders recess area (brake cleaner / towels).


Apply light lubricant to HPFP cylinder recess wall (oil / finger).
Note: Light lubricant will help with installation of dome in upcoming step.


Mount pistons.
Insert 2 sets of piston spring, piston, and piston cap in cylinders.
Lightly lubricate piston before mounting (oil / fingers).

Press down and release each piston to verify reciprocation movement.

Installation of dome


Mount swash plate onto pistons.
Orient swash plate with side large holes centered at front (picture).
Place swash plate on pistons. Swash plate will tilt down to rear (picture).
Verify piston cap tops are matted flat with swash plate.


Install new dome sealing O-ring.
Insert O-ring (inner side) in O-ring cavity on one side of dome, then stretch O-ring and drop into cavity on opposite side dome (fingers).
Verify O-ring is not twisted.


Lightly lubricate dome sealing O-ring (oil / fingers).


Place dome over swash plate.
Tilt dome to rear and mount over swash plate (picture).
Verify dome mounts correctly over swashplate with swashplate shaft at center and protruding from dome cylinder (picture).

Clean 2 dome mounting brackets and 3 dome mounting bolts (brake cleaner / towels).


Install dome small mounting bracket.
Place bracket, with rolled outer edges up, onto dome and rotate to align bolt mounting holes with HPFP body bolt mounting holes (picture).
Note: Bracket and bolt holes are symmetrical, thus there is no special rotational position.


If N14, Install dome large mounting bracket.
Orient bracket with bolt holes recesses up and long extension to rear of HPFP (picture).
Place bracket onto dome and align bolt mounting holes with lower bracket and HPFP body bolt mounting holes (picture).


Initially install dome bracket 2 rear mounting bolts.
Align bracket(s) and initially thread bracket 2 rear mounting bolts (T20 torx bit socket 1/4" w/ 3/8” to 1/4” socket adapter).

Note: Present step picture and following three step pictures only show N14 with second bracket. N18 is same steps but without second bracket.


Install dome bracket front mounting bolt.
Press down on bracket front and initially thread front bolt (T20 torx bit socket 1/4" w/ 3/8” to 1/4” socket adapter).


Tighten dome mounting bolts.
Evenly tighten 3 dome mounting bolts to evenly draw dome to HPFP body (T20 torx bit socket 1/4" w/ 3/8” to 1/4” socket adapter / 3/8” ratchet).

Note: Full bolt tightening is performed in next step.


Fully tighten dome mounting bolts
Fully tighten 3 dome mounting bolts, 10 Nm (7 ft-lb) (T20 torx bit socket 1/4" w/ 3/8” to 1/4” socket adapter / 3/8” torque wrench).
Note: Tighten bolts in multiple passes. Verify one pass with all bolts fully tightened.

Filling of HPFP with oil


Mount swashplate shaft socket.
Place socket on swashplate shaft and hit multiple times to press onto shaft ~3mm (~1/8”) (picture) (10mm 12 point 1/2” socket / 2lb sledgehammer).


Rotate swashplate to verify pistons function and to assess socket straightness.
Turn swashplate socket clockwise approximately two rotations (hand).
Note: Piston parts may initially stick. Rotating swashplate should rectify any issues.
Hit socket tool from top as needed to rectify its straighten to shaft (2 lb sledgehammer).
Note: Precise straightness of socket is not necessary.


Fill pouring cup ~1/4 (2 oz) full with HPFP oil (CHF 11S).


Pour HPFP oil into HPFP.
Mate pouring cup to swashplate socket and pour oil into dome.
Fill oil until oil rises to almost top of dome cavity. Exact level is not important.
Note: ~.23 oz of oil is filled. Exact amount is not important.

Filled oil will drain into HPFP and air bubbles will rise up.
This will take time and patience.


Repeat above step of filling oil and draining into HPFP.
On ~7th fill, oil will not drain into HPFP (picture).


Mount driver on swashplate socket (impact driver & 1/2" socket adapter).


Spin swashplate socket clockwise until oil drains into HPFP (impact driver & 1/2" socket adapter).
Note: Initially spin swashplate slowly and then increase speed steadily until oi drains into HPFP.
Note: Remaining oil at bottom of cavity will need to be left without spinning to slowly bubble and drain into HPFP.


Repeat above step of filling oil and draining into HPFP by spinning swashplate socket.
On the ~7th fill and spin (~13 total fills), oil will not drain into HPFP (picture).
Note: Once emptied, fill pouring cup ~1/8 (2 oz) full with HPFP oil (CHF 11S).
Note: HPFP will take ~3/8 cup (3 oz) oil in total.

Verify oil will not further drain into HPFP by spinning swashplate at tool highest speed (impact driver). This verifies HPFP is full of oil and all air is removed.


Remove swashplate shaft socket.
Pull and tilt socket in multiple directions until socket comes off shaft (1/2” ratchet).

Installation of rotary seal and rotor


Mount new rotary seal onto swashplate shaft (picture).


Place rotary seal punch tool upside down on rotary seal (picture).
Center punch inner diameter on seal inner seal lip (feel).


Hit rotary seal punch to press rotary seal into HPFP dome (2 lb sledgehammer).
Repeat hit until punch fully mattes with dome top surface.


Remove rotary seal punch and inspect rotary seal.
Rotary seal will be installed even with dome top (picture).

Clean HPFP dome area (brake cleaner / towels).


Place rotary seal punch tool right side up on rotary seal (picture).
Center punch inner diameter on rotary seal inner seal lip (feel).


Hit rotary seal punch to press rotary seal into HPFP dome (2 lb sledgehammer).
Repeat hits until punch inserts ~1mm into dome (picture).


Remove rotary seal punch and inspect rotary seal.
Rotary seal top will be installed ~1mm into dome and be located at end of dome inner diameter chamfer (bevel) (picture).

Clean HPFP dome area (brake cleaner / towels).


Place rotor on swashplate shaft with tabs pointing down (picture).


Hit rotor several times to initially install rotor on swashplate shaft (2 lb sledgehammer).


Remove HPFP from vise jaws and place on vise anvil with rotor at top (picture).


Strike rotor repeatedly to install onto swashplate shaft (2lb sledgehammer).
Note: Progressive significant striking will be needed to fully install rotor on shaft.
Rotor is fully installed once swashplate shaft reaches top of rotor (picture).

Note: Indentations from rotor installation will be found on dome (picture).


Install new dome O-ring.
Clean O-ring mounting location (brake cleaner / towels).
Mount O-ring on one side of dome, then stretch O-ring and mount on opposite side of dome (fingers).
Push O-ring down into mounting cavity (fingers).
Verify O-ring is not twisted.


Lightly lubricate dome O-ring (oil / fingers).

Installation of solenoid


Inspect solenoid O-rings.
Notice small O-ring at end, medium O-ring at middle, and large O-ring at base.
Notice small O-ring is installed adjacent to plastic washer and O-ring is positioned closer to solenoid end than plastic washer.


Remove solenoid O-rings.
Pry, stretch, pull, and roll 3 O-rings off solenoid (pick tool).

Clean solenoid (brake cleaner / towels).


Install new O-rings on solenoid.
Stretch, roll and mount 3 O-rings on solenoid.
Position small O-ring at end groove, medium O-ring at middle groove, and large O-ring at base.
Small O-ring mounts closer to end than associate plastic washer.
Slide large O-ring to base.

Verify O-rings are not twisted and small and medium O-rings are at approximate center of groove.

Apply light lubricant to 3 solenoid O-rings (oil / fingers).


Place HPFP on vise anvil with solenoid port at top and rear (picture).
Rotate rotor to contact vise for better stability (picture).


Mount solenoid on HPFP.
Orient solenoid connector to rear left and insert solenoid in HPFP (picture).


Mount solenoid bracket on solenoid.
Orient solenoid bracket with indentation to bottom and slot to rear left and slide over solenoid (picture).


Mount solenoid bolts.
Insert 2 solenoid bolts in solenoid bracket and align bracket and initially thread bolts into HPFP (fingers).


Tighten 2 solenoid bolts evenly to draw solenoid evenly to HPFP (T25 torx bit socket 1/4" w/ 3/8” to 1/4” socket adapter / 3/8” ratchet).

Fully tighten 2 solenoid bolts, 12 Nm (8 ft-lb) (T25 torx bit socket 1/4" w/ 3/8” to 1/4” socket adapter / 3/8” torque wrench).

Remove HPFP from vise.
